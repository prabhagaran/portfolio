Boards inside a battery rack live next to a DC bus that can exceed 1000 V. Getting spacing wrong isn't a signal-integrity bug — it's a fire. Here is the checklist I apply to every high-voltage layout before it goes to fab.

## Know your working voltage, not your nominal

Spacing is driven by the **maximum working voltage** between two conductors, including transients. A "48 V" string in a 16-string rack can put hundreds of volts between adjacent measurement channels during a fault. Build a voltage map of the board first; the schematic won't do it for you.

## Creepage vs. clearance

- **Clearance** is the shortest distance through air. It protects against flashover and scales with overvoltage category and altitude.
- **Creepage** is the shortest distance along the surface. It protects against tracking and scales with pollution degree and the CTI of your laminate.

Creepage is almost always the binding constraint on FR-4. IEC 60664-1 tables give the numbers; for a pollution degree 2 environment and material group IIIa, expect roughly 6.3 mm at 630 V working voltage.

## Layout techniques that buy margin

1. **Routed slots** under optocouplers and across isolation boundaries — a 1 mm slot resets the creepage path.
2. **Guard the boundary** — keep a silkscreen-marked keep-out band with no copper, vias, or test points.
3. **One boundary, one crossing** — every isolation crossing (isoSPI transformer, digital isolator, isolated DC-DC) should be visible in a single column on the board.
4. **Conformal coating** helps pollution degree, but only if your process controls coverage — don't claim it in the safety file unless production can prove it.

## Review like an auditor

Print the layout 2:1, draw the isolation boundary in red, and measure every crossing with the ruler tool. Ten minutes of this has caught more safety issues in my designs than any DRC deck.
