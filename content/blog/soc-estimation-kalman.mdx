State of charge is the one number everyone asks the BMS for and the one number it can't measure. You infer it — and the quality of that inference decides whether your pack strands its user at "20%" or quietly over-discharges at "5%".

## Why coulomb counting alone drifts

Integrating current is exact in theory and leaky in practice:

- **Sensor offset** integrates linearly into SoC error — 50 mA of offset on a 100 Ah pack is ~1.2% per day.
- **Current-dependent gain error** shows up on every fast charge.
- **Coulombic efficiency** isn't 1.0, and it changes with temperature and age.

Coulomb counting gives you excellent short-term *shape* and unbounded long-term *drift*.

## What the OCV curve gives back

Open-circuit voltage maps to SoC well — but only at rest, and for LFP chemistry the curve is so flat between 20% and 80% that a millivolt of measurement error is several percent of SoC. So OCV correction gives you bounded long-term accuracy and useless short-term dynamics: the exact complement of coulomb counting.

## The EKF, demystified

An extended Kalman filter is just a principled way to blend the two: propagate SoC with the current integral, then correct it with the voltage prediction error from an equivalent-circuit model (typically 2RC). The tuning lives in two covariance matrices — trust in the model vs. trust in the voltage measurement.

On a Cortex-M4F, a 3-state EKF per pack runs comfortably under 50 µs per step at 10 Hz. The real cost is not CPU — it's **characterization**: you need OCV curves and RC parameters across temperature, which means weeks of cell testing before the first line of filter code matters.

## What I actually ship

A hybrid: coulomb counting as the backbone, EKF correction gated by data quality (rested cells, valid temperature range, sane innovation), and hard recalibration at full charge. Boring, explainable, and it holds ±3% over a full season of field data — which is what the warranty team actually asked for.
